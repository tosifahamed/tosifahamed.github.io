I am currently a Theory Fellow at HHMI's Janelia Research Campus and will be joining the Department of Bioengineering at the Indian Institute of Science and Technology, Bangalore in August 2026.


From the flickering of neural activity to the unfolding of gene regulatory programs to the flux of metabolites in the bloodstream, biological systems exhibit complex, multi-scale dynamics that have long resisted quantitative understanding. Recent technological advances have changed this, enabling detailed measurements at high spatial and temporal resolution across all of these domains. My research focuses on the inverse problem this creates: given rich experimental measurements of animal movement, neural activity, gene expression, or physiological state, how do we infer the dynamical principles that generated them? I develop mathematical and computational tools, drawing on dynamical systems theory, operator-theoretic methods, and machine learning, to make this inference tractable across biological domains.

The problems that will define my research going forward include how populations of neurons encode and process complex, unpredictable sensory stimuli; how brain and body coordinate during physiological perturbations such as sickness, using large-scale neural recordings as a window into this reorganization; and how gene expression landscapes evolve over space and time during embryonic development, inferred from single-cell RNA sequencing and cell-tracking data in C. elegans embryos. Across all of these, the objective is the same: to build mathematical frameworks that are grounded in experimental data and reveal principles that would otherwise remain invisible.

Behavioral dynamics from data

(With Antonio Costa and Greg Stephens) Dynamical systems analysis of C. elegans locomotion revealed that the dynamics could be captured by a 7-dimensional attractor. Study of the spectrum of Lyapunov exponents showed an intriguing mathematical structure, where behavioral variability shows signs of chaotic dynamics with a damped-driven Hamiltonian structure.

Extrasynaptic signaling enables an asymmetric juvenile motor circuit to produce a symmetric gait

(With Yangning Lu and Zhen Lab) The first-stage larval C. elegans worm has an unusually asymmetric motor circuit, where muscles on only one side receive direct excitation from motor neurons. Using electron microscopy, calcium imaging, genetic perturbation, optogenetics, and computational modeling, we showed that extrasynaptic volume transmission provides the excitation necessary for muscle contraction on the other side.

Maximally predictive ensemble dynamics from data

(With Antonio Costa, David Jordan and Greg Stephens) Animal behavior, like language, has structure across multiple scales. We proposed a solution using operator-theoretic methods: learning an operator representation of the dynamics to build a maximally predictive state-space and decompose hidden scales via the operator eigenvectors.
